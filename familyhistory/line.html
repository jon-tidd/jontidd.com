I
c. 1600 England – 1656 Woburn, Massachusetts

John Tidd, the immigrant

⚭ Margaret · tailor · founder of Woburn

Sailed in 1637, signed Woburn's Town Orders in 1640, rang the meeting-house bell, sergeant of the train band, and in 1653 one of the "bold petitioners" for Christian Liberty. The whole American line starts with him.

II
c. 1618 England – 13 April 1703 Lexington · his stone still stands

John Tidd

⚭ Rebecca Wood, Woburn 1650 (d. 1717, aged 92)

Crossed from Yarmouth at nineteen. In 1686 he bought the Fiske homestead and moved the family to Cambridge Farms, the village that became Lexington: an assessor, a subscriber to the first meeting-house, and the reason the next two centuries of the line are Lexington ones. He and Rebecca lie side by side in the Old Burying Ground.

📖 Hudson, p. 696
III
20 January 1662 Woburn – 26 December 1730 Lexington · his stone still stands

Joseph Tidd

⚭ Mary · constable 1699 · selectman 1714

The first of the line born to Woburn and buried at Lexington. Constable and selectman of the young town; the double slate stone he shares with his wife Mary stands a few steps from his parents' graves. The 1729 laying-out of "the way leading from Concord to Woburn" ran through his Cambridge Farms world.

📖 Hudson, p. 697
IV
15 May 1707 – 18 September 1773 Lexington

Joseph Tidd

⚭ Dorothy Stickney of Woburn (1712–1790)

Selectman of Lexington in 1761, 1766 and 1767, with lands in five towns: Lexington, Woburn, New Braintree, Templeton and Phillipston. His will, proved 1772, parcelled that world out among the sons, including a farm lot for the youngest, John, who would need it sooner than anyone knew.

📖 Hudson, p. 697 🪦 Family plot
V
26 October 1749 – 29 March 1812 Lexington · his stone still stands

John Tidd, minuteman of Lexington Green

⚭ Elizabeth Reed of Woburn, 1778 (d. 1799)

He stood on Lexington Common at dawn on April 19, 1775, in Captain Parker's company, at the opening fight of the American Revolution. Among the last to leave the Green, he was ridden down by a British officer, struck with a sword, robbed while he lay senseless, and left for dead. He lived another thirty-seven years. The 1782 deed on the Archive page, "I John Tidd of Lexington… with Elizabeth my now married wife," is in his own hand.

He was a member of Captain Parker's company. Among the last to leave the Common he was pursued by a British officer on horseback and struck down by a sword; while senseless the British robbed him and left him for dead.Hudson, History of the Town of Lexington · Genealogies, p. 698

VI
2 March 1779 – 9 January 1842 Lexington · his stone still stands

John Tidd

⚭ Esther Haywood (1781–1852)

Born four years after his father bled on the Green, he kept the Lexington homestead through the young republic's first half-century. He is the "Grandfather Tidd of Lexington" of Aunt Esther's letters; his brother Jacob became a merchant in Boston, and Jacob's son took the Boston School Medal of 1828 that Esther still treasured ninety years later.

📖 Hudson, p. 700
VII
6 January 1807 – 27 April 1880 Lexington · his stone still stands

Charles Tidd, the schoolmaster

⚭ Rebecca M. Nourse of Waterford, Maine, 1830 (d. 1847), the writer of the 1840 letter

Town clerk of Lexington 1832–1838, on the school committee for years, and a teacher for more than thirty years. His wife Rebecca wrote the family's 1840 letter on anti-slavery stationery, counting the four Lexington men who dared vote "Freedom's ticket." When Charles died, the town voted its formal Resolutions by a rising vote, unanimously: "He was emphatically a good man and a worthy citizen." Their daughter Esther, born five months after the letter, became the family's first historian.

📖 Hudson, p. 700
VIII
12 February 1838 Lexington – 13 December 1899 Palmer, Nebraska

Charles Lowell Tidd

⚭ Helen A. Gookin of Portsmouth, N.H., 1866

"Master Charley" of his mother's 1840 letter, "determined that every body shall submit to his authority" at age two. He answered a different authority at twenty-four: Company A, 48th Massachusetts Infantry, in the Civil War his parents' movement had foretold. Then he took the line west, an early settler of Palmer, Nebraska, where he opened one of the town's first stores. His sister Esther's letters followed the family west for the rest of her life.

📖 Hudson, p. 700
IX
c. 1867 – c. 1930 Palmer, Nebraska

The Palmer rancher name under research

son of Charles Lowell · father of Charles William

The one rung of the ladder still missing its first name. He carried on the Palmer store his father founded, ran a thousand-acre wheat ranch at Grainton, kept a house in Long Beach, remarried late in life, and died about 1930. Grandpa Joy's letter remembers the estate, the ranch and "Old Nell"; the Merrick County records that will give his name are the next stop for the family historian.

🔍 Research open

The Palmer estate

Joy Thomas Tidd, "A Letter to Jonathan and Stephen," 2004

"Ever since grandfather Tidd had died, I think that was 1930, Helen [dad's sister] had haggled over the estate. Dad gave her the house in Long Beach, and Old Nell [grandfather Tidd's second wife] had come in for a third of the estate, and got the beautiful home in Palmer. Grandpa had married her the year before. All that was left was the wheat ranch at Grainton. I think it was about 1,000 acres… But it was the depression. Dad wanted to weather the storm. He felt after the depression it would be worth something again. Was he right!!"

X
1892 Palmer, Nebraska – 1935 near Lyman, Nebraska

Charles William Tidd

⚭ "Tops" · storekeeper of Huntley, Wyoming

Born at Palmer, educated at the University of Nebraska, a bank man, then a storekeeper on the high plains. The family lived at Ceres, California, where Aunt Esther's letters reached him, "Dear Charles," and where Joy was born, before the store at Huntley, Wyoming, and the hard Depression years that ended his life. Grandpa Joy's letter faces all of it with open eyes, and with the two farewell letters his father left.

🪦 Find a Grave

The tent behind the store

Joy Thomas Tidd, "A Letter to Jonathan and Stephen," 2004

"To regress, I was born in Ceres in 1922 and arrived in Huntley at the age of two. The store building was built but that first year we spent some time living in a tent behind the store. It was sort of rough, there was no water, gas, or electricity… We had a model T Ford pick-up that cousin Delores would drive down to the community well to pick up water. The means of conveyance was a skid with a 55 gallon drum mounted amidship. We would rumble down to the well with kids hanging from every corner."

XI
born 1922 Ceres, California

Joy Thomas Tidd, the family historian

Grandpa Joy · the reason this site exists

From a tent behind a Wyoming store to a long life of study. In the 1990s he assembled the research that mapped this whole line, and in 2004 he set down his life and his thinking in a hundred-page letter to his grandsons Jonathan and Stephen. His polaroid of a Lexington church, matched on Google Street View, is what led Jon and Kathleen to the family stones in 2010.

A letter to Jonathan and Stephen

Joy Thomas Tidd, 2004 · 100+ pages, held by the family

A hundred pages of memoir, science, scripture and poetry, written for his grandsons. It moves from a Wyoming boyhood ("I was born in Ceres in 1922…") through war, family, the cosmos and the Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam, and it preserves the family's memory of Palmer, the store, the ranch, and the people this page is built from.
